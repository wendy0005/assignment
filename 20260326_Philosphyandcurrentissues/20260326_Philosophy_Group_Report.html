    • A. Introduction of sexual abuse

    Sexual abuse is a critical and multifaceted social issue that continues to challenge the moral and ethical foundations of Malaysian society. It is defined not merely as a criminal act, but as a profound violation of human dignity and autonomy. Within the context of Malaysia’s national philosophy, sexual abuse represents a failure to uphold the principles of Kesopanan dan Kesusilaan (Good Behavior and Morality) as outlined in the Rukun Negara.

    In recent years, the landscape of this issue has shifted significantly due to rapid urbanization, the digital revolution, and the social stresses brought about by the COVID-19 pandemic. Despite increased awareness campaigns and legislative updates, such as the Sexual Offences Against Children Act (SOACA), statistics show a concerning upward trend in reported cases. This report seeks to provide a comprehensive analysis of the systemic causes of sexual abuse—ranging from power imbalances to social learning—and evaluate its devastating impacts on the individual survivor and the broader community. By applying sociological theories and philosophical lenses, we aim to propose integrated solutions that foster a safer, more ethical environment for all citizens.

    • C. Definition of the terms

  • Sexual Abuse: Ontologically defined as any sexual act occurring without the explicit, clear, and voluntary consent of a participant. It includes physical force, threats, and psychological manipulation (cyber-grooming).
  • Consent: The outward manifestation of internal autonomy—a "voluntary yes" expressed without coercion.
  • Karamah Insaniah: A concept within the National Education Philosophy referring to "human dignity/nobility," which must be instilled to prevent the objectification of others.
  • Cyber-Grooming: A method of obtaining sexual access through online deception, tricks, and emotional manipulation.

    • D. Causes of sexual abuse (Layman’s Summary)

    Power Gaps and the Betrayal of Trust One of the primary causes of sexual abuse is an imbalance of power within social and institutional structures. As explained by Finkelhor (2009), abuse is often a way for someone in a position of authority—like a teacher, a relative, or an employer—to exploit those who are younger or "lower" in status.

  • Real-Life Example: In Malaysia, a significant majority of abuse cases are perpetrated by individuals known to the victim. Statistics from the Royal Malaysia Police (PDRM) show that cases of incest and abuse by relatives remain a persistent issue, where the predator exploits the victim's dependency and trust (PDRM, 2022; RSIS International, 2023).

    • Learned Behavior and Societal Roles Abusive behavior is often acquired through the observation and imitation of others (Social Learning Theory, Bandura, 1977). This is reinforced by outdated societal views where one gender is seen as superior, leading to a mindset of control rather than mutual respect.

  • Real-Life Example: Research suggests that pervasive exposure to pornography from a young age among Malaysian youth can shape a distorted "script" of dominance and submission. This normalizes non-consensual behavior and makes it harder for individuals to recognize the importance of healthy boundaries (Noor & Rahman, 2018).

    • Environmental Stress and Online Risks External life struggles like poverty and lack of supervision create conditions where abuse can thrive. In the modern world, this risk has moved online through "cyber-grooming."

  • Real-Life Example: In 2024 alone, Malaysia recorded as many as 16,000 reports of Child Sexual Abuse Material (CSAM). Predators often use social media platforms like WeChat to trick children as young as seven years old into dangerous digital relationships that can later lead to physical assault (Safe Online, 2023; PDRM, 2024).

    • The Culture of Silence and Institutional Gaps Abuse persists when there is a month of oversight and a culture of shame. In many communities, the fear of "malu" (shame) or "rape myths" keeps victims from speaking up.

  • Real-Life Example: The culture of victim-blaming is a major barrier to justice in Malaysia. Public attitudes often place the "burden" on the victim to avoid abuse through their dress or behavior, leading many survivors to suffer in silence rather than report the incident to the police for fear of not being believed (WAO, 2021; Astro Awani, 2022).

    • E. Impacts of sexual abuse on the society

    Sexual abuse has profound and far-reaching consequences that extend beyond individual survivors to families, institutions, and society as a whole. At the personal level, survivors often experience long-term psychological trauma, including depression, anxiety, post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), and feelings of guilt or shame. These impacts can disrupt education, employment, and relationships, reducing overall quality of life. Physical health may also be affected, with survivors facing increased risks of chronic illness, substance abuse, and reproductive health complications.

    The effects ripple outward into families and communities. Survivors’ trauma can strain family cohesion, erode trust, and create cycles of silence or stigma that discourage disclosure. Communities may struggle with the social costs of untreated trauma, including reduced productivity and increased healthcare burdens. Institutions that fail to protect individuals or respond adequately to abuse face a loss of public confidence, damaging their credibility and legitimacy.

    On a societal scale, sexual abuse undermines social cohesion and imposes significant economic costs through healthcare, legal processes, and lost productivity. Yet, survivors’ resilience and advocacy have also driven systemic reforms, highlighting the importance of prevention, accountability, and support services. Addressing sexual abuse requires not only strong law enforcement but also comprehensive education, survivor-centered care, and institutional transparency.

    Ultimately, the impacts of sexual abuse demonstrate that it is not merely a private issue but a societal crisis demanding collective responsibility and sustained action.

    F. Statistic of sexual abuse in Malaysia

    Based on the statistics, there was a noticeable decline in reported sexual harassment cases between 2016 and 2018. This reduction may be attributed to increased law enforcement efforts, public awareness campaigns, and stronger institutional measures aimed at addressing sexual misconduct. However, from 2018 onwards, the number of cases began to rise again, reaching significant peaks in 2021 and 2023.

    One possible explanation for this upward trend is the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic. During periods of lockdown, individuals were confined to their homes, which may have heightened risks of domestic abuse and harassment. Additionally, the stress and isolation associated with the pandemic could have exacerbated harmful behaviors. It is also important to note that greater awareness and advocacy during this period may have encouraged more survivors to come forward and report incidents, contributing to the increase in recorded cases.

    Overall, while enforcement and awareness efforts have shown some effectiveness, the data highlights the need for sustained preventive strategies, stronger support systems for survivors, and continued education to reduce sexual harassment in Malaysia.

    H. Analysis of sexual abuse: Social Learning Theory

    Social Learning Theory, developed by Albert Bandura (1977), posits that behavior is not purely instinctive but is acquired through the observation, imitation, and reinforcement of models within one's environment. In the context of sexual abuse, this theory provides a profound explanation for how abusive cycles are sustained within a society.

    Observation and the "Script" of Dominance In environments where patriarchal norms are strictly enforced, individuals may observe role models who equate masculinity with dominance and control. This "script" is often reinforced by exposure to harmful media and pornography, which often depicts sexual encounters as acts of power rather than mutual respect. When young people see these behaviors modeled without consequence, they "learn" that aggression is an acceptable way to interact in intimate relationships.

    Reinforcement and the Absence of Punishment Bandura’s theory also emphasizes reinforcement. If an individual observes that abusive behavior leads to a desired outcome (such as control over a partner) and is not met with social or legal punishment, the behavior is reinforced. In Malaysia, the culture of "malu" (shame) and the low reporting rate act as a form of negative reinforcement for predators. Because the risk of being caught is perceived as low, the "lesson" learned is that abuse is a viable tool for exerting dominance.

    Intergenerational Cycles Finally, Social Learning Theory explains the tragic "cycle of abuse" where children who witness or experience abuse at home are at a higher risk of becoming perpetrators or victims themselves later in life. They have been conditioned to see these toxic dynamics as the "normal" standard for relationships. Therefore, breaking this cycle requires a radical shift in the "models" provided to youth through comprehensive sexuality education and the promotion of the "Balanced Human" (JERIS) concept in schools.
