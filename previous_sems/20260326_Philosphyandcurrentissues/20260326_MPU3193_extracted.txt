E. Impacts of sexual abuse on the society
Sexual abuse has profound and far-reaching consequences that extend beyond individual
survivors to families, institutions, and society as a whole. At the personal level, survivors often
experience long-term psychological trauma, including depression, anxiety, post-traumatic stress
disorder (PTSD), and feelings of guilt or shame. These impacts can disrupt education,
employment, and relationships, reducing overall quality of life. Physical health may also be
affected, with survivors facing increased risks of chronic illness, substance abuse, and
reproductive health complications.
The effects ripple outward into families and communities. Survivors’ trauma can strain
family cohesion, erode trust, and create cycles of silence or stigma that discourage disclosure.
Communities may struggle with the social costs of untreated trauma, including reduced
productivity and increased healthcare burdens. Institutions that fail to protect individuals or
respond adequately to abuse face a loss of public confidence, damaging their credibility and
legitimacy.
On a societal scale, sexual abuse undermines social cohesion and imposes significant
economic costs through healthcare, legal processes, and lost productivity. Yet, survivors’
resilience and advocacy have also driven systemic reforms, highlighting the importance of
prevention, accountability, and support services. Addressing sexual abuse requires not only
strong law enforcement but also comprehensive education, survivor-centered care, and
institutional transparency.
Ultimately, the impacts of sexual abuse demonstrate that it is not merely a private issue
but a societal crisis demanding collective responsibility and sustained action.
F. Statistic of sexual abuse in malaysia
Based on the statistics, there was a noticeable decline in reported sexual harassment cases
between 2016 and 2018. This reduction may be attributed to increased law enforcement efforts,
public awareness campaigns, and stronger institutional measures aimed at addressing sexual
misconduct. However, from 2018 onwards, the number of cases began to rise again, reaching
significant peaks in 2021 and 2023.
One possible explanation for this upward trend is the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic.
During periods of lockdown, individuals were confined to their homes, which may have
heightened risks of domestic abuse and harassment. Additionally, the stress and isolation
associated with the pandemic could have exacerbated harmful behaviors. It is also important to
note that greater awareness and advocacy during this period may have encouraged more
survivors to come forward and report incidents, contributing to the increase in recorded cases.
Overall, while enforcement and awareness efforts have shown some effectiveness, the
data highlights the need for sustained preventive strategies, stronger support systems for
survivors, and continued education to reduce sexual harassment in Malaysia.

Hari Prasad Govinda ( 4 Journal)
A review of academic journals, Malaysian report and news articles shows that sexual abuse is a
complex issue influenced by cultural, institutional, and psychological factors.
First, Finkelhor (2009) explains that sexual abuse is strongly linked to power imbalance and lack
of supervision. Individuals who hold authority—such as parents, relatives, teachers, or
employers—are more likely to exploit their position when there is minimal oversight. This is
especially relevant in Malaysia, where many cases occur within familiar environments. The
study highlights that abuse is rarely random; instead, it often happens in situations where
perpetrators have easy access to victims and can manipulate or control them.
Secondly , research by Noor & Rahman (2018) in Malaysia highlights that many sexual abuse
cases involve perpetrators who are known to the victim, such as family members or
acquaintances. This finding is consistent with reports from The Star (2023), which stated that
most abuse cases occur in familiar environments rather than by strangers. This challenges the
common misconception that sexual abuse is mainly caused by unknown offenders.
Thirdly, Ahmad et al. (2020) examined the psychological effects of sexual abuse among
Malaysian survivors and found that victims commonly suffer from long-term trauma, including
depression, anxiety, and low self-esteem. These findings are aligned with global studies such as
WHO (2021), which emphasize that sexual abuse has lifelong mental health consequences.
In addition, several studies suggest that education plays a crucial role in prevention. Research by
Rahman and Karim (2022) shows that individuals who receive proper education about consent,
boundaries, and personal safety are less likely to become victims. Awareness programs in
schools and communities help individuals recognise abusive behaviour and take appropriate
action.
H. Analyze of sexual abuse
Social Learning Theory, developed by Bandura (1977), explains that individuals acquire
behaviour through observation, imitation, and reinforcement within their social
environment. In the context of sexual abuse, this theory suggests that individuals who are
exposed to violent or inappropriate behaviour—whether in the family, community, or
media—may learn and replicate such actions. For instance, repeated exposure to
pornography or harmful online content can gradually normalise aggressive or
non-consensual behaviour, shaping an individual’s understanding of relationships and
consent. Furthermore, when abusive actions are not punished or are ignored, they may be
reinforced and repeated over time. A person who grows up in an environment where
abuse is present or tolerated may begin to perceive such behaviour as acceptable or
normal. Therefore, Social Learning Theory highlights the importance of preventive
measures such as promoting positive role models, providing education on consent and
healthy relationships, and regulating exposure to harmful media content. These efforts
can help reduce the likelihood of individuals adopting abusive behaviours (Bandura,
1977).
