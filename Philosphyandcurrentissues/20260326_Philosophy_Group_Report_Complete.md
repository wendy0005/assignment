### A. Introduction of sexual abuse
Sexual abuse is a critical and multifaceted social issue that continues to challenge the moral and ethical foundations of Malaysian society. It is defined not merely as a criminal act, but as a profound violation of human dignity and autonomy. Within the context of Malaysia’s national philosophy, sexual abuse represents a failure to uphold the principles of *Kesopanan dan Kesusilaan* (Good Behavior and Morality) as outlined in the *Rukun Negara*. 

In recent years, the landscape of this issue has shifted significantly due to rapid urbanization, the digital revolution, and the social stresses brought about by the COVID-19 pandemic. Despite increased awareness campaigns and legislative updates, such as the Sexual Offences Against Children Act (SOACA), statistics show a concerning upward trend in reported cases. This report seeks to provide a comprehensive analysis of the systemic causes of sexual abuse—ranging from power imbalances to social learning—and evaluate its devastating impacts on the individual survivor and the broader community. By applying sociological theories and philosophical lenses, we aim to propose integrated solutions that foster a safer, more ethical environment for all citizens.

### C. Definition of the terms
*   **Sexual Abuse:** Ontologically defined as any sexual act occurring without the explicit, clear, and voluntary consent of a participant. It includes physical force, threats, and psychological manipulation (cyber-grooming).
*   **Consent:** The outward manifestation of internal autonomy—a "voluntary yes" expressed without coercion.

### D. Causes of sexual abuse (Layman’s Summary)

**Power Gaps and the Betrayal of Trust**
One of the primary causes of sexual abuse is an imbalance of power within social and institutional structures. As explained by **Finkelhor (2009)**, abuse is often a way for someone in a position of authority—like a teacher, a relative, or an employer—to exploit those who are younger or "lower" in status. 
*   **Real-Life Example:** In Malaysia, a significant majority of abuse cases are perpetrated by individuals known to the victim. Statistics from the Royal Malaysia Police (PDRM) show that cases of incest and abuse by relatives remain a persistent issue, where the predator exploits the victim's dependency and trust (**PDRM, 2022; RSIS International, 2023**).

**Learned Behavior and Societal Roles**
Abusive behavior is often acquired through the observation and imitation of others (**Social Learning Theory, Bandura, 1977**). This is reinforced by outdated societal views where one gender is seen as superior, leading to a mindset of control rather than mutual respect.
*   **Real-Life Example:** Research suggests that pervasive exposure to pornography from a young age among Malaysian youth can shape a distorted "script" of dominance and submission. This normalizes non-consensual behavior and makes it harder for individuals to recognize the importance of healthy boundaries (**Noor & Rahman, 2018**).

**Environmental Stress and Online Risks**
External life struggles like poverty and lack of supervision create conditions where abuse can thrive. In the modern world, this risk has moved online through "cyber-grooming."
*   **Real-Life Example:** In 2024 alone, Malaysia recorded as many as 16,000 reports of Child Sexual Abuse Material (CSAM). Predators often use social media platforms like WeChat to trick children as young as seven years old into dangerous digital relationships that can later lead to physical assault (**Safe Online, 2023; PDRM, 2024**).

**The Culture of Silence and Institutional Gaps**
Abuse persists when there is a lack of oversight and a culture of shame. In many communities, the fear of "malu" (shame) or "rape myths" keeps victims from speaking up.
*   **Real-Life Example:** The culture of victim-blaming is a major barrier to justice in Malaysia. Public attitudes often place the "burden" on the victim to avoid abuse through their dress or behavior, leading many survivors to suffer in silence rather than report the incident to the police for fear of not being believed (**WAO, 2021; Astro Awani, 2022**).

### H. Analysis of sexual abuse: Social Learning Theory
Social Learning Theory, developed by Albert Bandura (1977), posits that behavior is not purely instinctive but is acquired through the observation, imitation, and reinforcement of models within one's environment. In the context of sexual abuse, this theory provides a profound explanation for how abusive cycles are sustained within a society.

**Observation and the "Script" of Dominance**
In environments where patriarchal norms are strictly enforced, individuals may observe role models who equate masculinity with dominance and control. This "script" is often reinforced by exposure to harmful media and pornography, which often depicts sexual encounters as acts of power rather than mutual respect. When young people see these behaviors modeled without consequence, they "learn" that aggression is an acceptable way to interact in intimate relationships.

**Reinforcement and the Absence of Punishment**
Bandura’s theory also emphasizes reinforcement. If an individual observes that abusive behavior leads to a desired outcome (such as control over a partner) and is not met with social or legal punishment, the behavior is reinforced. In Malaysia, the culture of "malu" (shame) and the low reporting rate act as a form of negative reinforcement for predators. Because the risk of being caught is perceived as low, the "lesson" learned is that abuse is a viable tool for exerting dominance.

**Intergenerational Cycles**
Finally, Social Learning Theory explains the tragic "cycle of abuse" where children who witness or experience abuse at home are at a higher risk of becoming perpetrators or victims themselves later in life. They have been conditioned to see these toxic dynamics as the "normal" standard for relationships. Therefore, breaking this cycle requires a radical shift in the "models" provided to youth through comprehensive sexuality education and the promotion of the "Balanced Human" (JERIS) concept in schools.

### J. Conclusion
In conclusion, sexual abuse in Malaysia is a multifaceted crisis that reflects a deep imbalance in the intellectual, emotional, and spiritual development of the community. Our analysis has shown that abuse is rarely a random occurrence; rather, it is a consequence of systemic exploitation of power, the persistence of outdated patriarchal hierarchies, and the acquisition of aggressive behaviors through social learning. Furthermore, the digital frontier has introduced new risks like cyber-grooming, which exploit the gap in digital literacy between generations.

To move forward, Malaysia must look beyond legal deterrence alone. While strong laws are essential, we must realign our societal values toward the realization of the "balanced human" as defined by the National Education Philosophy (FPK). This requires a collective commitment to transparency in our institutions, the decolonization of sexual morality, and the promotion of *karamah insaniah* (human dignity). Only through a combination of robust legal enforcement, comprehensive education, and an end to the "culture of silence" can we hope to protect the vulnerable and ensure that every individual is treated with the respect they deserve.
