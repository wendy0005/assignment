### Slide 4

Systems Analysis and Design (SAD)

Systems Analysis and Design (SAD) is a structured approach to developing information systems that meet business needs.

It involves two main activities: analysis, which focuses on understanding problems, requirements, and the existing system; and design, which translates those requirements into a blueprint for building the new system.

SAD helps bridge the gap between users and developers by using models, diagrams, and methodologies to ensure the final system is effective, efficient, and user-friendly.

It is a core skill for IT students, as it teaches students how to transform business requirements into practical technology solutions.

### Slide 26

FEASIBILITY ANALYSIS

Feasibility analysis or Feasibility Study is an assessment of the practicality of a proposed plan, project or method

Feasibility analysis guides the organization in determining whether to proceed with a project.

Feasibility analysis also identifies the important risks associated with the project that must be managed if the project is approved.

### Slide 27

FEASIBILITY ANALYSIS (cont)

As with the system request, each organization has its own process and format for the feasibility analysis, but most include techniques to assess four areas:

Technical feasibility

Economic feasibility

Organizational feasibility

Schedule feasibility

The results of evaluating these four feasibility factors are combined into a feasibility study deliverable that is submitted to the approval committee at the end of project initiation.

### Slide 28

Technical Feasibility

Technical feasibility is the extent to which the system can be successfully designed, developed, and installed by the IT group.

It is, in essence, a technical risk analysis that strives to answer the question: “can we build it?”

Risks can endanger the successful completion of a project. The following aspects should be considered:

Users’ and analysts’ should be familiar with the application.

Familiarity with the technology

Project size

Compatibility of the new system with the technology that already exists

### Slide 29

Economic Feasibility

Economic feasibility analysis is also called a cost-benefit analysis, that identifies the costs and benefits associated with the system.

This attempts to answer the question: “should we build the system?”

### Slide 30

CASH FLOW ANALYSIS AND MEASURES

IT projects involve an initial investment that produces a steam of benefits over time, along with some on-going support costs.

Cash flows, both inflows and outflows, are estimated over some future period.

Simple cash flow projection

### Slide 31

ORGANIZATIONAL FEASIBILITY

Organizational feasibility of the system is how well the system ultimately will be accepted by its users and incorporated into the ongoing operations of the organization.

There are many organizational factors that can have an impact on the project, and seasoned developers know that organizational feasibility can be the most difficult feasibility dimension to assess.

In essence, an organizational feasibility analysis is to answer the question “if we build it, will it benefit the organisation?”

### Slide 32

ORGANIZATIONAL FEASIBILITY (cont)

One way to assess the organizational feasibility is to understand how well the goals of the project align with the business objectives and organizational strategies.

A second way to assess the organizational feasibility is to conduct stakeholder analysis.

### Slide 36

SCHEDULE FEASIBILITY

Schedule feasibility assesses whether a proposed project can be completed within the required timeframe. It examines the project timeline, deadlines, resources, and dependencies to determine if the system can realistically be delivered on time.

Key Question: “Can we finish the project within the deadline?”

Factors Considered: project complexity, team size and skills, available technology, and external constraints (e.g., regulatory or market deadlines).

Risk: If the timeline is unrealistic, it may lead to rushed work, poor quality, or even project failure.

Ensures that a consistent reproducible approach is applied to all projects

Reduces the risks associated with shortcuts and mistakes

Produces complete and consistent documentation from one project to the next

As development teams and staff change over time, the result of prior work can be easily retrieved, understood and modified as required

The M:N relationship means that many instances of a parent entity can relate to many instances of a child entity.

Object-oriented systems focus on capturing the structure and behavior of information systems in little modules (objects) that encompass both data and processes.

The object-oriented approaches can use any of the traditional methodologies (e.g., waterfall development, parallel development, etc.), and are most associated with an iterative development RAD methodology.

### Slide 5

Unified Modeling Language (UML)

5

Unified Modeling Language (UML) is a standard set of diagramming techniques that provide a graphical representation to model systems development project.

Object-oriented systems analysis and design use UML to depict a system.

UML has different diagrams to show various views of the system.

It is a blueprint that supports software engineering activities from analysis to implementation.

UML models can be used during requirements gathering, system design, and even system maintenance.

### Slide 6

Benefits of Using UML

6

UML provides a common visual language that can be understood by developers, analysts, designers, and even non-technical stakeholders.

It makes it easier to analyze and communicate system requirements, as diagrams can simplify complex information.

Helps with design validation before any code is written, saving time and effort later in the development cycle.

Enables early detection of design flaws and inconsistencies.

Supports Object-Oriented Analysis and Design (OOAD), enabling teams to design reusable, modular, and maintainable software.

UML is tool-independent—meaning it can be applied in Agile, Waterfall, or hybrid project methodologies.

### Slide 7

Unified Modeling Language Version 2.0

The objective of the Unified Modeling Language (UML) is to provide a common vocabulary of object-oriented terms and diagramming techniques.

The diagrams are broken into two major groupings:

Structure diagrams - for representing data and static relationships in an information system. It describes what exists in the system rather than how it behaves.

Behavior diagrams - for representing dynamic relationships among the objects. It illustrate how the system behaves over time—how components interact and how processes unfold.
