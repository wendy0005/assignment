## BCL1123 - Internet of Things - Topic 1: 1 Topic Overview

Topic Overview: A History and Overview of IoTIntroductionThe Internet of Things (IoT) is built on the Internet, embedded development, edge networks, sensors, cloud platforms, and cloud services. It aims to construct a smart world where everything is connected. In this era of the Internet of Everything (IoE), communication extends beyond humans to devices that can receive and process data automatically.Did you know? The origin of the IoT can be traced back to 1991 when scientists at Cambridge University installed a portable camera next to a coffee pot to check if the coffee was ready, saving them trips downstairs.Development HistoryThe term "Internet of Things" was first coined in 1999 by Kevin Ashton, focusing heavily on RFID technologies. Since then, the adoption of smartphones, smart home devices, and connected embedded systems (2016-present) has made IoT a fundamental technology.IoT Architecture LayersModern IoT systems operate on a layered architecture:Sensing Layer: Collects information and processes signals using sensors, RFID, and GPS.Network Layer: Transmits information via the Internet, 5G, Wi-Fi, and NB-IoT.Platform Layer: Integrates massive information resources into large intelligent networks (Cloud, Data Storage).Application Layer: Provides specific services for users, such as Smart Grids, Smart Transportation, and Smart Logistics.Recent Developments (Post-2022)One of the most significant recent developments in the IoT landscape is the integration of Artificial Intelligence, often referred to as AIoT. Instead of sending all data to a centralized cloud, AI models now process data directly on the IoT devices (Edge AI). This minimizes latency, enhances data privacy, and is critical for real-time applications like autonomous vehicles and predictive maintenance in smart manufacturing (SmartDev, 2024).

IoT Development History & Technological MilestonesThe Internet of Things (IoT) represents a monumental leap in how the physical and digital worlds interact. It is built upon the foundation of the Internet, embedded development, edge networks, highly sensitive sensors, massive cloud platforms, and sophisticated cloud services. The ultimate goal of IoT is to build a "smart world" where everything is connected.In this era, often referred to as the Internet of Everything (IoE), communication is no longer just human-to-human or human-to-machine. It is fundamentally about devices. Devices can now receive, transmit, and process data automatically. Simply put, IoT is an Internet dedicated to thing-to-thing connections.1.1 The Early Origins (1990 - 1991)While the term "IoT" feels remarkably modern, its conceptual origins date back to the early 1990s in academic environments where programmers sought to solve minor, everyday inconveniences using network connectivity.1990 - The Carnegie Mellon Vending Machine: The earliest recorded instance of an internet-connected appliance was a Coca-Cola vending machine at Carnegie Mellon University. Programmers modified the machine to detect exactly how many cans were left and whether they were cold, saving them a trip down the hall.1991 - The Trojan Room Coffee Pot: At the Computer Laboratory in Cambridge University, scientists faced a similar problem: walking downstairs only to find an empty coffee pot. To solve this, they wrote a program, installed a portable camera next to the coffee pot, and utilized image capture technology to check the coffee level remotely over their local network.Figure 1: Historical Milestones - The 1990 Vending Machine and the 1991 Coffee Pot.1.2 Formalizing the Concept (1999)As technology matured—specifically Radio-Frequency Identification (RFID) and the Electronic Product Code (EPC)—the concept moved from academic novelty to industrial potential. In 1999, the term "Internet of Things" was officially coined by Kevin Ashton, who envisioned a world where computers could capture data about physical objects without human intervention.1.3 National Policies and Massive Scaling (2009 - 2015)The recognition of IoT as a critical national infrastructure occurred in the late 2000s. In August 2009, China's former Premier Wen Jiabao delivered a landmark speech during an inspection in Wuxi city, stressing the need to establish "Sensing China." The Layered Architecture of an IoT SystemTo manage the immense complexity of connecting billions of diverse devices, IoT relies on a highly structured layered architecture. The widely accepted standard (often referred to in the Huawei "1+2+1" solution) consists of four primary layers: Sensing, Network, Platform, and Application.3.1 The Sensing Layer (The Foundation)The sensing layer is the physical foundation of the IoT. Its primary function is to collect information from the physical world and process analog signals into digital data. This layer utilizes sensing and identification technologies (like RFID, GPS, and environmental sensors) to enable inanimate objects to "speak."Huawei LiteOS: In the context of the sensing layer, operating systems must be incredibly efficient. Huawei LiteOS is a lightweight IoT OS developed specifically for this layer. Its kernel can be as small as 6 KB, and it features an "OpenCPU" architecture designed for devices with limited hardware resources (like water meters or gas meters). It supports secure device-cloud interconnection and Software over-the-air (SOTA) differential upgrades.3.2 The Network Layer (The Link)The network layer acts as the vital link between the physical sensors and the cloud. It directly accesses and transmits information collected by the sensing layer using the Internet, mobile communications networks (such as 4G, 5G, and NB-IoT), and satellite communications.NB-IoT (Narrowband IoT): Proposed heavily by companies like Huawei and Vodafone, NB-IoT is an end-to-end solution perfectly suited for transmitting small amounts of data over long distances with minimal power consumption. Meanwhile, 5G divides networks into "slices" to handle UHD high-bandwidth applications or real-time ultra-low latency services.3.3 The Platform Layer (The Brain)The platform layer is the brain of the IoT ecosystem. Situated above the network layer and below the application layer, it integrates massive information resources into a cohesive intelligent network.It solves highly complex data problems: Storage (managing massive databases), Retrieval (search engines), Use (data mining and machine learning analysis), and Protection (data security and privacy). The Huawei Cloud IoT platform provides device access, security verification, and service orchestration at this level.3.4 The Application Layer (The Interface)The application layer is the user interface of the IoT system. It receives processed information from the platform layer, makes actionable decisions, and provides specific services to end-users (e.g., smart grid management, smart logistics dashboards). Furthermore, it can send instructions back down through the network layer to physically control devices at the sensing layer.Figure 3: The 4 Logical Layers of IoT Architecture.Review Question: ArchitectureWhich layer of the IoT architecture is responsible for utilizing data mining and machine learning to process massive amounts of integrated information?A) The Sensing LayerB) The Network LayerC) The Platform LayerD) The Application LayerCheckpoint 3: I understand the 4-layer IoT architecture and the specific role of each layer.4. IoT Engineering: Roles, Responsibilities, and TrendsIoT Engineering is a highly multidisciplinary field that blends computer science, information and communications engineering, microelectronics, detection automation, and instrument science. IoT engineers are responsible for the end-to-end research, design, testing, and maintenance of IoT architectures, platforms, chips, and sensors.4.1 The Three Main Career PathsThe future of IoT engineering is incredibly promising, with professionals generally specializing in one of three main paths:IoT Embedded Development: This is hardware-centric. Engineers here focus on perceptual control, microcontrollers, IoT application protocols, and low-level networking/communication. They work directly with the Sensing Layer.IoT Application Development: This is software-centric. Engineers develop IoT platform applications, IoT edge computing system algorithms, and IoT mobile applications. They operate primarily within the Platform and Application Layers.IoT System Integration and Management: This is operations-centric. Responsibilities include physical IoT device installation, commissioning, system deployment, operation and maintenance (O&M), and technical consulting for enterprises.Advanced Industry Trend: AIoT and Edge AI (2024 & Beyond)While the standard 4-layer architecture relies heavily on the Platform Layer (Cloud) for processing, a massive paradigm shift is occurring with the convergence of Artificial Intelligence and IoT, known as AIoT.Instead of transmitting all raw data through the Network Layer to the Cloud for analysis, Edge AI involves running machine learning models directly on the devices at the Sensing or Network Edge. This shift provides three critical benefits:Zero Latency: Crucial for time-sensitive applications like autonomous vehicles, where waiting for a cloud response could cause a crash.Bandwidth Reduction: Only sending the *results* of the analysis to the cloud, rather than gigabytes of raw video/sensor data.Enhanced Privacy: Sensitive data (like facial recognition in a smart home) is processed locally and never leaves the device.Reflective Task: The Impact of Edge AIAssume you are working in IoT Embedded Development. Internet of Vehicles (IoV) and C-V2XThe Internet of Vehicles (IoV) is the convergence of mobile communications and the automotive industry. The ultimate goal of IoV is to enable fully autonomous driving and zero-collision roadways.4.1 Challenges in the Automotive IndustryTraffic Congestion: Rapidly increasing vehicle ownership outpaces road construction, leading to severe congestion and wasted time in urban centers.Road Safety: Human error (fatigue, distraction) remains the leading cause of traffic accidents. Improving safety requires reducing reliance on manual driving.Emissions & Pollution: Inefficient routing, idling in traffic jams, and fossil fuel consumption heavily impact urban air quality and global climate goals.4.2 Basic IoV Concepts & Autonomous Driving (SAE Levels)IoV relies on a multi-layered architecture connecting the vehicle, road infrastructure, network, and cloud. A core objective of IoV is achieving advanced autonomous driving, defined by the Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) across 6 levels:Level 0 (No Automation): Zero autonomy; the driver performs all tasks.Level 1 (Driver Assistance): Vehicle controls either steering OR acceleration/braking (e.g., adaptive cruise control).Level 2 (Partial Automation): Vehicle controls both steering AND acceleration/braking simultaneously, but the driver must monitor the environment at all times.Level 3 (Conditional Automation): The vehicle can drive itself under specific conditions (e.g., highway). The driver can look away but must be ready to take over within a limited time when prompted.Level 4 (High Automation): The vehicle can drive itself completely in specific geofenced areas. No driver intervention is required under those conditions.Level 5 (Full Automation): The vehicle can drive itself anywhere, in all conditions. No steering wheel or pedals are required.4.3 IoV DevelopmentIoV communication technologies have evolved rapidly to meet the stringent latency and reliability requirements of autonomous driving:DSRC (Dedicated Short-Range Communications): An older, Wi-Fi-based standard (802.11p) initially proposed for vehicular networks but limited by short range and interference.LTE-V (4G Era): Brought vehicular communication into the cellular domain, improving range and establishing the foundation for modern IoV.C-V2X (5G NR Era): Cellular Vehicle-to-Everything. Leverages 5G networks to provide ultra-reliable low-latency communication (URLLC), supporting advanced L4/L5 autonomous use cases.4.4 V2X (Vehicle-to-Everything) CommunicationsAccording to the Huawei IoT architecture, V2X covers multiple driving scenarios (as mapped out in Figure 4.1) to enable full collaboration between pedestrians, vehicles, roads, and clouds:V2V (Vehicle-To-Vehicle): Aims to implement collision warning and congestion avoidance. Vehicles communicate directly (without speakers) to share positioning, distance, direction, and speed in real time.V2I (Vehicle-To-Infrastructure): Enables vehicles to exchange data with roadside infrastructure like traffic lights, road signs, and roadblocks. For example, roadside units provide the remaining time of traffic lights and speed limits to help vehicles drive smoothly and find obstacles early.V2P (Vehicle-To-Pedestrian): Aims to prevent collisions by detecting pedestrians through information interconnection, utilizing terminals like a pedestrian's mobile phone, tablet, or wearable device.Cloud & Internal: Direct communication with the cloud helps vehicles learn about macroscopic traffic jams to change paths. Internally, sensors (tire pressure, speed, tilt angle) communicate continuously with the vehicle's controller device.4.5 IoV-Powered Smart IntersectionsA smart intersection uses IoV to eliminate blind spots and optimize traffic flow. Road Side Units (RSUs) mounted on traffic lights process data from cameras and radar. They broadcast this data to approaching vehicles, warning them of pedestrians around corners, optimizing green-light speeds, and allowing emergency vehicles to preempt traffic signals.4.6 DRIS (Digital Road Infrastructure System)DRIS focuses on upgrading the physical road into a digital asset. By embedding sensors (weigh-in-motion, meteorological sensors) into the pavement and combining them with edge computing, DRIS creates a "Digital Road Twin." It provides real-time, lane-level environmental and traffic conditions directly to autonomous vehicles, serving as an intelligent co-pilot for the car.
